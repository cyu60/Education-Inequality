Title

Importance & Inherency of the Extracurricular Gap

Among the myriad of structural, intractable, and complex problems that dominate the American education landscape, access to extracurricular activities stands as one of the largest gaps between the haves and have-nots. Low-income and minority students are at a structural disadvantage when it comes to accessing extracurricular activities, being three times less likely to participate in after-school programs[1]. Research has documented a consistently positive relationship between extracurricular participation and educational backgrounds[2]. More specifically, a student's participation in extracurricular activities has been linked to self-confidence, commitment to school values, heightened educational expectations, improved grades, higher standardized test scores, better cognitive & noncognitive skills, retention, and long-term outcomes such as college enrollment and college completion[3].



Importance of STEM Extracurricular

The United States Bureau of Labor Statistics expects STEM occupations to grow by 8% between 2019 and 2029, compared to 3.4% for non-STEM occupations[4]. Wages for STEM occupations are also estimated to be more than double those of non-STEM, potentially providing a pathway to financial independence for students from low-income communities.

Research shows that the experience students have with STEM in extracurricular settings can have a profound effect on their decision to pursue STEM in college or as a career. Students benefit immensely from the opportunity to engage in hands-on, informal STEM learning in environments that provide encouragement [5]. Over the last decade, after school programs have become a crucial means of access to these sorts of environments.

Furthermore, after school extracurriculars in STEM are providing learning opportunities in demographic groups traditionally underrepresented in STEM.

Moving Forwards

It is clear that the need for afterschool STEM extracurriculars exists, while at the sametime barriers to participation have increased. Taken together, these facts indicate that there needs to be greater investment in extracurricular STEM from the federal, state, and local level. Both public and private investment are needed now more than ever to provide the diversity of experiences students need to inspire learning and success, both in school and in life.
