Educational Inequality

Mathias Insley, Keidai Lee, Chinat Yu, Pinhong He

The Extracurricular Gap

It’s no secret: many problems permeate American education, and access to extracurricular activities is one of the largest inequities present. Low-income and racial minority students are at a structural disadvantage when it comes to accessing extracurricular activities, being three times less likely to participate in after-school programs[1]. Research has documented a consistently positive relationship between extracurricular participation and educational backgrounds[2]. More specifically, a student's participation in extracurricular activities has been linked to self-confidence, commitment to school values, heightened educational expectations, improved grades, higher standardized test scores, better cognitive & noncognitive skills, retention, and long-term outcomes such as college enrollment and college completion[3]. The COVID-19 pandemic has further illustrated and perpetuated the worsening disparities in access to extracurricular activities[4].

Why STEM Extracurriculars?

The United States Bureau of Labor Statistics expects STEM occupations to grow by 8% between 2019 and 2029, compared to 3.4% for non-STEM occupations[5]. Wages for STEM occupations are also estimated to be more than double those of non-STEM, potentially providing a pathway to financial independence for students from low-income communities.



Research shows that the experience students have with STEM in extracurricular settings can have a profound effect on their decision to pursue STEM in college or as a career. Students benefit immensely from the opportunity to engage in hands-on, informal STEM learning in environments that provide encouragement [6]. Over the last decade, after school programs have become a crucial means of access to these sorts of environments.

Moving Forwards

While it is clear that the need for after school STEM extracurriculars exists, barriers to participation have increased. Thus, these facts indicate that there needs to be greater investment in STEM extracurriculars from the federal, state, and local level. Schools do more than just provide academics: they are also spaces for social and emotional support, safety, food systems, and health care systems[8]. Both public and private investment in STEM extracurriculars are needed now more than ever: students need a diversity of experiences inspiring learning and success, both in school and in life.
